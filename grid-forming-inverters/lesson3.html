1. Key Characteristics of GFMs

  • Voltage Source Behavior: GFMs regulate their terminal voltage directly
  • Autonomous Operation: Capable of operating without grid support
  • Synchronization-Free: No PLL required for phase-locking
  • Inertia Emulation: Some implementations mimic the inertia of synchronous machines

2. Main Control Objectives

  • Voltage Regulation: Maintain voltage amplitude and waveform integrity
  • Frequency Regulation: Adjust frequency based on power imbalances
  • Power Sharing: Share load among multiple GFMs using droop characteristics
  • Fault Support: Remain connected and stable during disturbances

3. Comparison with Grid-Following Inverters

  • GFLs inject current based on sensed voltage, requiring a strong grid
  • GFMs set voltage and provide stability even in weak or isolated grids
  • GFMs support black-start and microgrid formation, GFLs do not

‚úÖ Summary

  • GFMs are voltage-regulating, autonomous, and synchronization-free
  • Their control targets include voltage/frequency regulation, stability, and sharing
  • They play a critical role in renewable-dominated and islanded power systems
