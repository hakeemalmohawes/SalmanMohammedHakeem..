As renewable energy sources replace fossil-fuel-based generation, synchronous machines‚Äîtraditionally the backbone of grid stability‚Äîare being retired. This leads to reduced inertia, voltage support, and fault handling. Without intervention, power systems become more fragile and harder to control.

‚ö° 2. GFMs as a Replacement Backbone

GFMs offer a pathway to restore stability and functionality in a grid dominated by inverter-based resources. Acting as virtual synchronous machines, they provide synthetic inertia, regulate frequency, stabilize voltage, and contribute to fault response. These features are essential to maintain a reliable and resilient grid.

üåç 3. Case Study: 100% Inverter-Based Island

Several pilot projects have demonstrated GFMs powering entire grids without synchronous machines. One example is the use of battery-based GFMs in island communities where diesel generators have been fully displaced. The GFMs maintain stable voltage and frequency while coordinating multiple renewables.

üìä 4. System Planning Considerations

  • GFMs must be strategically placed to maintain stability zones
  • Control parameters must be tuned for coordination and power sharing
  • Hybrid setups with GFLs benefit from a minimal share of GFMs acting as anchors

‚úÖ Summary

  • GFMs are essential to support high-renewable, low-inertia power systems
  • They provide critical grid services previously handled by synchronous generators
  • Real-world deployments confirm the feasibility of inverter-only operation
  • Planning, placement, and tuning are key to successful system integration
