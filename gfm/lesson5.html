üìä 1. Transient and Dynamic Response

Grid-forming inverters play an essential role in maintaining the dynamic stability of a power system. When the load suddenly increases or generation fluctuates, GFMs must respond by adjusting their output frequency and voltage to stabilize the system. Their control systems are designed to react quickly and predictably to such changes, ensuring continuous operation.

ü§º 2. Virtual Inertia

Synchronous machines inherently provide inertia due to their rotating mass, which resists changes in frequency. GFMs replicate this effect using virtual inertia, implemented through control algorithms that slow the rate of frequency change in response to power imbalances. This helps prevent large frequency swings in low-inertia grids dominated by inverters.

üí™ 3. Damping Control

In addition to inertia, GFMs introduce damping, which mitigates oscillations in voltage and frequency. This damping is achieved by incorporating derivative terms or energy storage buffers into the control loop. The goal is to absorb and dissipate energy fluctuations before they destabilize the grid.

‚ö° 4. Comparison with GFL Inverters

Grid-following inverters (GFLs) do not contribute to system inertia or damping. They rely entirely on the grid reference and may even disconnect during disturbances. GFMs, in contrast, remain online, stabilize the system, and provide valuable dynamic support. This makes them a critical component of modern, inverter-rich power systems.

‚úÖ Summary

  • GFMs enhance grid stability during dynamic events like load changes or faults
  • Virtual inertia emulates generator mass to resist frequency deviations
  • Damping control suppresses power system oscillations
  • GFMs outperform GFLs in resilience and dynamic contribution
