1. The Need for Local Control

GFMs are designed to operate in systems where centralized control or strong interconnections are absent. They must regulate voltage magnitude and frequency locally, often based solely on their internal measurements and reference signals.

2. Droop Control Mechanisms

Much like synchronous generators, GFMs use frequency‚Äìpower (P‚Äìf) and voltage‚Äìreactive power (Q‚ÄìV) droop control to share load among multiple units. This ensures proportional power sharing and avoids conflict when several GFMs are operating in parallel.

3. Frequency Regulation

GFMs adjust their frequency output in response to changes in real power. A drop in frequency indicates more demand than generation, prompting the inverter to increase output. The inverse holds true during over-generation.

4. Voltage Regulation

Similarly, GFMs use reactive power measurements to maintain voltage levels. If voltage drops due to a reactive load, the GFM responds by supplying more reactive power, helping to keep the local voltage within acceptable limits.
