1. Importance of Experimental Validation

Simulation alone cannot guarantee reliable GFM behavior in real systems. Lab-scale experiments, power hardware-in-the-loop (PHIL), and field tests help verify stability, interoperability, and controller robustness under realistic conditions.

2. Hardware Architecture

  • DC Source: Usually a PV emulator, battery system, or programmable DC supply
  • Inverter Module: Implements control strategy (e.g., droop, VSM, dVOC)
  • Filters: LCL filters smooth switching harmonics
  • Load Bank: Adjustable for real/reactive power emulation
  • Measurement System: Voltage, current, and phase sensors for feedback

3. Power Hardware-in-the-Loop (PHIL)

PHIL integrates real inverter hardware into a simulated grid using a digital real-time simulator (DRTS). It enables repeatable testing with complex disturbances and allows tuning of control parameters before field deployment.

4. Field Demonstrations

Real-world trials of GFMs include islanded microgrids, solar+battery installations, and hybrid diesel-renewable systems. Demonstrations validate grid-forming capability, load sharing, black-start support, and fault ride-through performance.
