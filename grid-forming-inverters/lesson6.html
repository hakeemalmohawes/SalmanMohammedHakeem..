1. Voltage Stability Contributions

GFMs actively regulate their terminal voltage, which supports voltage stability across the network. Their ability to source and sink reactive power quickly helps mitigate voltage dips, particularly in weak grids or at long-distance feeders.

2. Frequency Stability and Inertia Emulation

In traditional grids, rotating mass provides inertia that buffers against sudden frequency changes. GFMs emulate this by adjusting their frequency setpoint dynamically in response to power imbalances, simulating the swing equation of synchronous machines.

3. Damping and Synchronization

Many GFM implementations incorporate virtual damping to suppress oscillations during transients. Their synchronization-free nature also avoids interaction delays that PLL-based systems suffer from, improving dynamic response and reducing grid fragility.

4. Supporting Grid Resilience

  • Enable black-start by establishing voltage and frequency
  • Remain stable and online during grid faults or low-inertia events
  • Coordinate with other GFMs to maintain smooth transitions
