‚öñÔ∏è 1. What Is a Grid-Following Inverter?

A Grid-Following Inverter (GFL) is a type of power electronic converter that injects electrical power into the grid by synchronizing its output current with the voltage waveform of the grid. It does this by sensing the grid voltage, using a phase-locked loop (PLL), and then regulating the injected current accordingly.

üåå 2. Key Characteristics

  • Acts as a current source, not a voltage source
  • Requires an existing grid voltage to operate
  • Dependent on a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) to stay synchronized

üìä 3. Role in Renewable Integration

GFL inverters are widely used in grid-tied solar PV, wind, and battery systems. They convert DC power from these sources to AC, matching the grid's voltage and frequency. Their control structure is well suited for stable, strong grids with sufficient inertia and grid-forming sources already present.

‚ö†Ô∏è 4. Limitations

  • Cannot operate in islanded or black start conditions
  • Reliant on the grid for synchronization and operation
  • May disconnect during faults or disturbances

‚úÖ Summary

  • GFL inverters follow the grid, injecting current based on sensed voltage
  • They are suitable for use in strong grids with traditional synchronous machines
  • GFLs are central to many modern renewable deployments
