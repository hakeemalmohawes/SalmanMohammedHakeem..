Grid-forming inverters are expected to stay connected and support the system during transient events. This behavior, known as fault ride-through, allows the GFM to maintain voltage and stabilize frequency even during faults. The GFM may reduce power output or switch to current-limited mode while maintaining synchronism.

During a disturbance, the GFM controller adjusts its output to remain within safe operating limits while contributing to system recovery. This may involve prioritizing frequency control, reducing reactive power output, or enabling dynamic current limitation based on fault type and location.

üö™ 4. Inverter-Compatible Protection

Because of their limited fault current, GFMs require new protection strategies:

  • Voltage-based protection: Uses under/over-voltage thresholds
  • Rate-of-change of frequency (ROCOF): Detects fast system events
  • Directional relays: Evaluate power flow for fault location
  • Communication-aided protection: Coordinates between relays and controllers

  • GFM fault current is limited and controlled
  • They support fault ride-through and grid stability
  • Traditional protection methods must be adapted for GFM behavior
  • Advanced, communication-based, and voltage-sensitive relays are essential
