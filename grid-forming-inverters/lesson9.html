1. Control Complexity

Implementing droop, VSM, or oscillator-based controls requires precise tuning and understanding of system dynamics. Improper configuration may lead to instability, poor power sharing, or harmonic distortion.

2. Lack of Standardization

Unlike synchronous generators, there are no universal standards or widely adopted grid codes for GFMs. This creates inconsistency in design, testing, and interoperability across vendors and utilities.

3. Cost and Converter Design

GFMs typically require high-performance converters, fast digital controllers, and robust protection schemes. These increase system cost compared to simpler GFL inverters. Thermal management and EMI filtering can also add complexity.

4. Grid Integration Risk

In multi-inverter systems, interactions between GFMs and GFLs can result in resonances or transient instability. Careful modeling, validation, and control design are necessary to avoid issues during high renewable penetration or islanded operation.
