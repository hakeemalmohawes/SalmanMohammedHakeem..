üîÑ 1. Self-Synchronization

One of the most important characteristics of a GFM inverter is its ability to self-synchronize. Unlike GFL inverters that require a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) to detect grid conditions, a GFM generates its own voltage waveform, making it the reference for other devices. This allows it to operate in islanded mode and support weak grids.

üöó 2. Voltage Source Behavior

GFMs behave as voltage sources with specified amplitude and frequency. They regulate these parameters autonomously and are capable of adjusting based on power flow, using droop or virtual oscillator control. This capability is crucial in scenarios where there is no dominant grid-forming source.

üìà 3. Inertia and Damping

Inverter-based systems do not have physical rotating mass, but GFM inverters can emulate virtual inertia and damping through control algorithms. This helps the grid resist frequency deviations and improves dynamic stability.

ü™ß 4. Fault Ride-Through and Grid Support

Unlike GFL inverters, which often disconnect during grid faults, GFMs are designed to ride through disturbances and provide support. They can inject current to support voltage or help with frequency stability during events like faults or load swings.

üìä 5. Black Start Capability

GFMs can energize a de-energized grid, a process known as black start. This is critical for system restoration after a major outage. Their ability to form a grid without external signals is what sets them apart from all other inverter types.

‚úÖ Summary

  • GFMs self-synchronize and act as voltage sources
  • They provide synthetic inertia and damping to stabilize the grid
  • They ride through faults and can perform black start
  • Their behavior is central to the future of stable inverter-dominated grids
