1. What Are Grid-Forming Inverters?

Grid-forming inverters (GFMs) are power electronic devices that can establish and regulate voltage and frequency within a power system. Unlike conventional inverters, which simply follow the grid, GFMs can set the voltage reference themselves, enabling them to operate independently or coordinate with other sources.

2. Motivation for Grid-Forming Control

As power systems transition to renewable energy, the share of synchronous machines is declining. These machines historically provided inertia, frequency stability, and voltage regulation. GFMs are proposed as substitutes for these critical services, especially in inverter-dominated grids where traditional mechanisms are inadequate.

3. Decline of Synchronous Machines

With the growing penetration of solar, wind, and storage systems, most new generation is interfaced via power electronics. These systems, if operated in a grid-following mode, cannot inherently provide frequency or voltage control. This makes the system vulnerable unless alternative approaches like GFMs are adopted.

4. System Reliability and Stability Concerns

Grid-following inverters rely on an existing voltage reference, which becomes unreliable or unavailable during disturbances. Grid-forming inverters, by contrast, can maintain voltage and frequency even in the absence of other sources, contributing to black-start capabilities, islanded operation, and overall system resilience.
