1. New Operational Paradigms

In a 100% renewable grid, operations must adapt to a system with less dispatchable generation and more weather-dependent sources. This means operators must rely more on forecasts, rapid flexibility, and automated decision-making.

2. Forecasting and Scheduling

Advanced forecasting of solar and wind is essential for day-ahead and intraday scheduling. These forecasts inform commitment of storage, flexible demand, and balancing resources to prepare for variability and uncertainty.

3. Real-Time Balancing

Real-time control becomes more dynamic. Fast-acting resources like batteries and demand response must respond to deviations from forecasts. Grid operators must monitor frequency and voltage stability closely and use digital tools for automatic corrections.

4. Reserves and Flexibility Services

  • Spinning reserve: Online resources that can increase output instantly
  • Non-spinning reserve: Resources that can be brought online quickly if needed
  • Frequency response: Automated, fast-acting support during disturbances
  • Ramp products: Support during rapid changes in renewable output

5. Control Centers and Grid Visibility

Operators use energy management systems (EMS) and distribution management systems (DMS) to maintain grid stability. Enhanced visibility into inverter-based generation and load behavior is critical to keeping the system reliable.
