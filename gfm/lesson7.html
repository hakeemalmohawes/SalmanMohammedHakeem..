Grid-Forming Inverters (GFMs) generate voltage and frequency, acting as voltage sources. They provide essential system services such as black start, voltage regulation, and inertia. Grid-Following Inverters (GFLs) rely on an existing voltage waveform to synchronize and inject current. They act as current sources and depend on a grid-forming reference to operate.

üåã 2. Control Approach

  • GFMs: Control voltage and frequency through droop or virtual oscillator control
  • GFLs: Use Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) to follow grid voltage and inject active/reactive power

üåê 3. Behavior During Disturbances

GFMs maintain voltage and ride through faults, actively supporting the system. GFLs often disconnect during faults or rely on protection to avoid damage. This makes GFMs far more suitable for low-inertia, unstable, or microgrid applications.

üåø 4. Application Scenarios

  • GFMs: Used in microgrids, black start units, and weak-grid support
  • GFLs: Common in large PV and wind farms where the grid is strong and stable

‚úÖ Summary

  • GFMs are voltage sources; GFLs are current sources
  • GFMs form the grid; GFLs follow the grid
  • GFMs offer better dynamic support and grid services
  • GFLs are cost-effective and widely deployed in strong grids
