1. Why Storage Matters

Renewable generation like solar and wind is not always available when needed. Energy storage allows us to save excess electricity when generation exceeds demand and release it when demand exceeds generation, helping to smooth variability and balance the grid.

2. Types of Energy Storage

  • Batteries: Lithium-ion batteries are widely used for fast, short-duration applications. Flow batteries and other chemistries are emerging for medium-duration use.
  • Pumped hydro: Water is pumped uphill and released to generate electricity later‚Äîideal for long-duration storage.
  • Thermal storage: Heat is stored for later use, often in concentrated solar plants or for industrial processes.
  • Hydrogen: Electricity is used to produce hydrogen, which can later be used as a fuel or reconverted to electricity.

3. Storage Applications

  • Frequency regulation: Quickly absorb or inject power to stabilize the grid
  • Peak shaving: Reduce demand during high-cost peak periods
  • Backup power: Provide energy during outages or system failures
  • Energy arbitrage: Buy low, store, and sell high when prices rise

4. Integrating Storage into the Grid

To maximize its benefits, storage should be integrated with grid operations, planning, and market participation. This includes enabling storage to provide ancillary services, reducing barriers to interconnection, and creating fair compensation models.
