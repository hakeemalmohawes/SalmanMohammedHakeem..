Grid-Forming Inverters (GFMs) actively regulate both voltage amplitude and frequency, unlike grid-following inverters that depend on externally measured signals. A GFM behaves as an AC voltage source, capable of independently establishing grid voltage at its terminals.

üìà 3. Power Sharing via Droop

In systems with multiple GFMs, droop characteristics ensure proportional power sharing. If two GFMs have identical droop settings, they naturally divide load based on their ratings. This method avoids the need for centralized dispatch or fast communication links.

üöÄ 4. Black Start and Islanding Capability

Because GFM inverters do not rely on a pre-existing voltage signal, they can start up and energize a de-energized grid, a feature known as black start. They can also seamlessly operate in islanded mode, maintaining local voltage and frequency without external references.

üß† 5. Mimicking Synchronous Generator Behavior

Droop control and other GFM strategies are inspired by the dynamics of synchronous machines. This analogy enables GFMs to integrate more naturally into power systems originally designed around rotating machinery. It also improves compatibility with protection schemes and system dynamics.
